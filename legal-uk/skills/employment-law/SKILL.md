## 1. Unfair Dismissal (ERA 1996 Part X)

### Qualifying Period

Under **s.108 Employment Rights Act 1996** ("ERA 1996"), an employee must have **2 years' continuous service** to bring an ordinary unfair dismissal claim. Continuous employment is calculated under ERA 1996 Part XIV, Chapter I (ss.210-219). A week that does not break continuity still counts towards the qualifying period.

### Fair Reasons for Dismissal (s.98)

The employer bears the burden of showing the reason (or principal reason) for dismissal falls within one of the following **potentially fair reasons** under s.98(1)-(2):

| Reason | Statutory Basis | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Capability or qualifications** | s.98(2)(a) | Poor performance, loss of professional licence |
| **Conduct** | s.98(2)(b) | Gross misconduct, persistent minor misconduct |
| **Redundancy** | s.98(2)(c) | Role eliminated, workplace closure |
| **Statutory illegality** | s.98(2)(d) | Driver loses driving licence |
| **Some other substantial reason (SOSR)** | s.98(1)(b) | Business reorganisation, reputational risk, refusal of new contractual terms |

Once a potentially fair reason is established, the tribunal applies the **s.98(4) reasonableness test**: did the employer act reasonably in treating the reason as sufficient for dismissal, having regard to equity and the substantial merits of the case? This is assessed within the **band of reasonable responses** — the tribunal must not substitute its own view for that of the employer (*Iceland Frozen Foods Ltd v Jones* [1983] ICR 17, EAT; affirmed *Foley v Post Office* [2000] ICR 1283, CA).

### Automatically Unfair Dismissals (No Qualifying Period)

Certain dismissals are **automatically unfair** regardless of length of service:

- **Pregnancy and maternity** — s.99 ERA 1996; reg.20 Maternity and Parental Leave etc. Regulations 1999
- **Whistleblowing** — s.103A ERA 1996 (protected disclosure under Part IVA)
- **Trade union membership or activities** — s.152 Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992 ("TULRCA 1992")
- **Health and safety** — s.100 ERA 1996 (raising health & safety concerns, acting as safety representative)
- **Asserting a statutory right** — s.104 ERA 1996
- **Jury service** — s.98B ERA 1996
- **Flexible working request** — s.104C ERA 1996
- **TUPE transfer** — reg.7 TUPE 2006 (where reason for dismissal is the transfer itself)

### Remedies

| Remedy | Details |
|---|---|
| **Reinstatement** (s.114) | Return to same job, same terms |
| **Re-engagement** (s.115) | Comparable or suitable employment |
| **Basic award** (s.119) | Calculated like statutory redundancy pay; capped at **£21,000** (from April 2025) |
| **Compensatory award** (s.124) | Capped at the **lower of £115,115 or 52 weeks' gross pay** (from April 2025). Uncapped for whistleblowing and certain health & safety dismissals |

The compensatory award is subject to the **Polkey reduction** (*Polkey v AE Dayton Services Ltd* [1988] ICR 142, HL) — a deduction for the chance the employee would have been fairly dismissed in any event. A further adjustment of up to **25%** may apply under s.207A TULRCA 1992 for unreasonable failure to follow the ACAS Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures.

## 2. Redundancy (ERA 1996 Part XI)

### Definition (s.139)

Redundancy arises where dismissal is wholly or mainly attributable to:

- **(a)** The employer has ceased or intends to cease carrying on the business, or carrying on the business in the place where the employee was employed; or
- **(b)** The requirements of the business for employees to carry out work of a particular kind have ceased or diminished or are expected to do so.

### Statutory Redundancy Pay Calculation (s.162)

Calculated by reference to the employee's **age**, **length of service** (capped at 20 years), and **weekly pay** (capped at **£700** from April 2025):

| Age Band | Entitlement per Complete Year |
|---|---|
| Under 22 | **0.5** week's pay |
| 22 to 40 | **1** week's pay |
| 41 and over | **1.5** weeks' pay |

**Maximum statutory redundancy pay** = 20 years × 1.5 × £700 = **£21,000**.

### Redundancy Pay Quick-Reference Table (April 2025 cap: £700/week)

| Years of Service | Age Under 22 | Age 22-40 | Age 41+ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | £700 | £1,400 | £2,100 |
| 5 | £1,750 | £3,500 | £5,250 |
| 10 | £3,500 | £7,000 | £10,500 |
| 15 | £5,250 | £10,500 | £15,750 |
| 20 | £7,000 | £14,000 | £21,000 |

### Consultation

**Individual consultation** is always required as part of a fair dismissal procedure. The employer must warn the employee, consult individually, and consider alternatives (redeployment). Failure to consult individually will almost certainly render the dismissal unfair (*Williams v Compair Maxam Ltd* [1982] ICR 156, EAT).

**Collective consultation** is required under **s.188 TULRCA 1992** where the employer proposes to dismiss **20 or more employees** at one establishment within 90 days:

| Number of Proposed Redundancies | Minimum Consultation Period |
|---|---|
| 20 to 99 | **30 days** before first dismissal takes effect |
| 100 or more | **45 days** before first dismissal takes effect |

Failure to comply: a **protective award** of up to 90 days' gross pay per affected employee (s.189 TULRCA 1992). The employer must also notify the Secretary of State via an **HR1 form** (s.193 TULRCA 1992).

### Selection Criteria

Selection must be based on **objective**, **measurable**, and **fair** criteria. Common criteria include:

- Last in, first out (LIFO)
- Skills, qualifications, and experience (skills matrix)
- Attendance/absence record (excluding disability-related and pregnancy-related absence)
- Disciplinary record
- Performance appraisals

## 3. Discrimination (Equality Act 2010)

### Protected Characteristics (s.4)

The Equality Act 2010 ("EqA 2010") identifies **9 protected characteristics**:

1. Age
2. Disability
3. Gender reassignment
4. Marriage and civil partnership
5. Pregnancy and maternity
6. Race
7. Religion or belief
8. Sex
9. Sexual orientation

### Types of Prohibited Conduct

| Type | Section | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct discrimination** | s.13 | Less favourable treatment *because of* a protected characteristic. Comparator required (actual or hypothetical). No justification defence except for age (if proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim). |
| **Indirect discrimination** | s.19 | Provision, criterion, or practice (PCP) that is applied universally but puts persons sharing a protected characteristic at a *particular disadvantage*. Defence: objective justification (proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim). |
| **Harassment** | s.26 | Unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic that has the purpose or effect of violating dignity or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment. |
| **Victimisation** | s.27 | Subjecting a person to a detriment because they have done a protected act (e.g., brought proceedings, given evidence, made an allegation under EqA 2010). |

### Reasonable Adjustments for Disability (ss.20-21)

Where a PCP, physical feature, or absence of an auxiliary aid puts a disabled person at a **substantial disadvantage**, the employer has a duty to take **reasonable steps** to avoid that disadvantage. Failure to comply constitutes discrimination. Factors in assessing reasonableness include cost, practicability, resources of the employer, and effectiveness of the step (*Environment Agency v Rowan* [2008] ICR 218, EAT).

### Worker Protection (Amendment of Equality Act 2010) Act 2023

From **26 October 2024**, employers owe a **proactive duty to take reasonable steps to prevent sexual harassment** of their employees in the course of employment (new s.40A EqA 2010). This includes third-party harassment. Where the duty is breached and a harassment claim succeeds, the Employment Tribunal may apply a **compensation uplift of up to 25%** (s.124(6A) EqA 2010). The Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) can enforce the preventive duty directly.

### Key Points

- No qualifying period for discrimination claims.
- Compensation is **uncapped** (no statutory ceiling on injury to feelings or financial loss).
- **Vento bands** for injury to feelings (as updated by *De Souza v Vinci Construction (UK) Ltd* [2017] EWCA Civ 879): Lower (£1,200-£11,200), Middle (£11,200-£33,700), Upper (£33,700-£56,200), with amounts above the upper band in exceptional cases. Updated annually by the Presidents of the Employment Tribunals.

## 4. Working Time (Working Time Regulations 1998)

The Working Time Regulations 1998 (SI 1998/1833) ("WTR 1998") implement the EU Working Time Directive (retained in domestic law post-Brexit).

| Right | Regulation | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Maximum weekly working time** | reg.4 | **48 hours** averaged over 17-week reference period. Worker may **opt out** in writing (reg.5). |
| **Annual leave** | reg.13 & 13A | **5.6 weeks** (28 days for a 5-day worker). Includes bank holidays at employer's discretion. |
| **Daily rest** | reg.10 | **11 consecutive hours** in each 24-hour period. |
| **Weekly rest** | reg.11 | **24 uninterrupted hours** in each 7-day period (or 48 hours in 14 days). |
| **Rest breaks** | reg.12 | **20 minutes** uninterrupted when daily working time exceeds 6 hours. |
| **Night work** | reg.6 | Average **8 hours** in any 24-hour period. Special hazards: absolute 8-hour limit. |

### Holiday Pay

Following *Harpur Trust v Brazel* [2022] UKSC 21, holiday pay for part-year workers must be calculated on average earnings over the 52-week reference period (disregarding weeks with no pay), not pro-rated. This applies under s.224 ERA 1996.

## 5. National Living Wage / National Minimum Wage (April 2025 Rates)

Rates set under the National Minimum Wage Act 1998 and National Minimum Wage Regulations 2015, as amended. Enforced by HMRC.

| Category | Hourly Rate (from April 2025) |
|---|---|
| **National Living Wage** (age 21 and over) | **£12.21** |
| **18 to 20 year olds** | **£10.00** |
| **Under 18** (above compulsory school age) | **£7.55** |
| **Apprentice rate** (under 19, or 19+ in first year of apprenticeship) | **£7.55** |
| **Accommodation offset** (daily limit) | **£10.66** |

### Key Compliance Points

- Salary sacrifice schemes, uniform costs, and certain deductions can reduce effective pay below NMW — employers must check net pay against the NMW floor.
- HMRC can issue **notices of underpayment**, impose **penalties of up to 200%** of the underpayment (capped at £20,000 per worker), and refer employers for **criminal prosecution** under s.31 NMW Act 1998.
- Workers can bring claims to the Employment Tribunal under s.11 NMW Act 1998 or s.13 ERA 1996 (unlawful deduction from wages).

## 6. TUPE (Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations 2006)

The Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations 2006 (SI 2006/246) ("TUPE 2006") protect employees when the business or service they work for transfers to a new employer.

### Scope

| Transfer Type | Regulation | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Business transfer** | reg.3(1)(a) | Transfer of an economic entity which retains its identity (*Spijkers v Gebroeders Benedik Abattoir CV* [1986] ECR 1119, CJEU). |
| **Service provision change** | reg.3(1)(b) | Activities carried out by one party are taken over by another (outsourcing, insourcing, retendering). Requires organised grouping of employees. |

### Key Protections

- **Automatic transfer of contracts**: reg.4 — employees transfer on existing terms and conditions. The new employer inherits all rights, powers, duties, and liabilities under the employment contract.
- **Dismissal connected to transfer**: reg.7 — a dismissal is **automatically unfair** if the sole or principal reason is the transfer itself. Exception: where there is an **economic, technical, or organisational (ETO) reason** entailing changes in the workforce (reg.7(2)-(3)).
- **Variations to terms**: Changes to terms connected to the transfer are **void** (reg.4(4)) unless there is an ETO reason.

### Information and Consultation (regs.13-16)

Both the old and new employer must inform and consult **appropriate representatives** (recognised trade union or elected employee representatives) **long enough before** the transfer to allow meaningful consultation. Required information includes the fact of transfer, date, reasons, legal/economic/social implications, and any measures envisaged.

Failure to comply: up to **13 weeks' pay** per affected employee as compensation (reg.15).

### TUPE Applicability Checker

When assessing whether TUPE applies, work through these questions:

1. Is there a transfer of a business or undertaking as a going concern? (Business transfer)
2. Alternatively, is there a service provision change — outsourcing, insourcing, or re-tendering of a contract?
3. Is there an organised grouping of employees whose principal purpose is to carry out the activities in question?
4. Are the activities fundamentally the same before and after the change?
5. Is this a single specific event or task, or a supply of goods only? (Excluded from SPC under reg.3(3))

If "yes" to questions 1 or (2, 3, and 4), and "no" to question 5, TUPE likely applies.

## 7. IR35 / Off-Payroll Working (ITEPA 2003 Chapter 10)

The off-payroll working rules (commonly "IR35") are found in **Chapter 10 of the Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003** ("ITEPA 2003"), as amended by Finance Act 2021.

### Who Assesses Status?

| Client Size | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Medium or large** (meeting 2 of 3: turnover > £10.2m, balance sheet > £5.1m, > 50 employees) | **Client** must determine status and issue a **Status Determination Statement (SDS)** |
| **Small** | **Worker's intermediary** (PSC) self-assesses |

### Status Determination Statement (SDS)

The client must take **reasonable care** in reaching the determination, provide the SDS to the worker and the fee-payer, and include reasons. The worker has a right to dispute the SDS, and the client must respond within 45 days.

### Key Factors for Employment Status

The following factors, drawn from case law (*Ready Mixed Concrete (South East) Ltd v Minister of Pensions* [1968] 2 QB 497; *Autoclenz Ltd v Belcher* [2011] UKSC 41), are used by HMRC's **CEST tool** (Check Employment Status for Tax):

| Factor | Points Towards Employment | Points Towards Self-Employment |
|---|---|---|
| **Control** | Client dictates what, how, when, where | Worker has autonomy over method and schedule |
| **Personal service** | Must perform work personally | Genuine right of substitution exercised in practice |
| **Mutuality of obligation** | Ongoing obligation to provide and accept work | No obligation beyond specific engagement |
| **Financial risk** | No financial risk; paid per hour/day | Bears risk of loss; invests own equipment |
| **Part and parcel** | Integrated into client's organisation | Operates independently, has own business structure |

### Penalties

If the off-payroll rules apply and are not followed, the fee-payer (or client, if no fee-payer in the chain) is liable for income tax, employee NICs, and employer NICs, plus potential penalties and interest under TMA 1970.

## 8. Flexible Working (Employment Relations (Flexible Working) Act 2023)

The Employment Relations (Flexible Working) Act 2023 amends s.80F ERA 1996 and came into force on **6 April 2024**.

### Key Changes

| Feature | Previous Position | From 6 April 2024 |
|---|---|---|
| **Eligibility** | 26 weeks' continuous service | **Day-one right** — no qualifying period |
| **Number of requests** | 1 per 12 months | **2 per 12 months** |
| **Decision period** | 3 months | **2 months** (unless extended by agreement) |
| **Consultation** | No requirement | Employer **must consult** before refusing |
| **Business reasons** | Employee must explain effect | Employee no longer required to explain effect on employer |

### Statutory Grounds for Refusal (s.80G(1)(b))

The employer may only refuse on one or more of these **8 business grounds**:

1. Burden of additional costs
2. Detrimental effect on ability to meet customer demand
3. Inability to reorganise work among existing staff
4. Inability to recruit additional staff
5. Detrimental impact on quality
6. Detrimental impact on performance
7. Insufficiency of work during the periods the employee proposes to work
8. Planned structural changes

If the employer refuses without consulting or outside the permitted grounds, the employee may complain to the Employment Tribunal (s.80H ERA 1996). Compensation: up to **8 weeks' pay**.

## 9. Employment Tribunal Procedure

### ACAS Early Conciliation (s.18A Employment Tribunals Act 1996)

Before presenting a claim to the Employment Tribunal, a prospective claimant **must** contact ACAS to start early conciliation ("EC"), unless an exemption applies (reg.3 Employment Tribunals (Early Conciliation: Exemptions and Rules of Procedure) Regulations 2014).

- ACAS has an initial period of **up to 1 month** to attempt conciliation.
- This can be extended by **up to 14 days** if ACAS considers there is a prospect of settlement.
- If conciliation fails, ACAS issues an **EC certificate** with a unique reference number required on the ET1 form.

### Time Limits

| Claim Type | Primary Limitation Period | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Unfair dismissal | **3 months minus 1 day** from effective date of termination | s.111(2) ERA 1996 |
| Wrongful dismissal (ET) | **3 months minus 1 day** from breach | Art.7 Employment Tribunals Extension of Jurisdiction (England and Wales) Order 1994 |
| Discrimination | **3 months minus 1 day** from act complained of | s.123(1) EqA 2010 |
| Unlawful deduction from wages | **3 months minus 1 day** from date of deduction (or last in a series) | s.23(2) ERA 1996 |
| Redundancy payment | **6 months** from relevant date | s.164(1) ERA 1996 |
| Equal pay | **6 months** from end of employment (in ET) | s.129 EqA 2010 |
| TUPE failure to inform/consult | **3 months minus 1 day** from date of transfer | reg.15(12) TUPE 2006 |

### ET Time Limit Calculator Logic

To calculate the deadline including the ACAS early conciliation extension:

1. **Start date (Day A)**: The date the ACAS EC notification was received by ACAS.
2. **End date (Day B)**: The date on the EC certificate.
3. **Original limitation date**: Calculate 3 months minus 1 day from the act/dismissal date.
4. **If the original limitation date falls between Day A and Day B**: The limitation date is extended to **Day B + 1 day**.
5. **If the original limitation date falls after Day B**: The limitation date is extended by the period from Day A to Day B (the "stop the clock" period).
6. **Minimum extension**: If the period between Day A and Day B is less than 1 month, the extension is treated as **1 month** from Day B.

**Example**: Dismissal on 15 January 2025. Original deadline: 14 April 2025. Day A: 1 March 2025. Day B: 1 April 2025 (31 days). Original deadline (14 April) falls after Day B, so add the Day A-to-Day B period (31 days) to the original deadline: new deadline = 15 May 2025.

### ET1 and ET3 Forms

- **ET1 (Claim Form)**: The claimant must include their name, respondent's details, nature of claim, remedy sought, and the ACAS EC certificate number. Filed online at the Employment Tribunal or by post.
- **ET3 (Response Form)**: The respondent has **28 days** from the date the ET1 was sent by the Tribunal to file a response. Late responses require an application for an extension under rule 20 of the Employment Tribunals Rules of Procedure 2013.

### Procedure After Filing

1. **Initial consideration** by an Employment Judge (rule 26)
2. **Case management** — preliminary hearing for directions, disclosure, witness statements
3. **Preliminary hearing** (open or private) — may determine jurisdiction or strike out
4. **Final hearing** — oral evidence, cross-examination, submissions
5. **Judgment** — with written reasons
6. **Remedies hearing** (if liability found and remedy not determined)
7. **Appeal** to the Employment Appeal Tribunal on a point of law only (s.21 Employment Tribunals Act 1996)

## 10. Whistleblowing (ERA 1996 Part IVA)

### Protected Disclosures (s.43B)

A qualifying disclosure is a disclosure of information which, in the reasonable belief of the worker, is made in the public interest and tends to show one or more of:

| Category | s.43B(1) |
|---|---|
| **(a)** Criminal offence | Has been, is being, or is likely to be committed |
| **(b)** Breach of legal obligation | Has been, is being, or is likely to be committed |
| **(c)** Miscarriage of justice | Has occurred, is occurring, or is likely to occur |
| **(d)** Danger to health & safety** | Of any individual |
| **(e)** Damage to the environment | Has occurred, is occurring, or is likely to occur |
| **(f)** Deliberate concealment | Of information tending to show any of the above |

### To Whom Disclosure May Be Made

The disclosure must be made to an appropriate person to qualify for protection:

- **Employer or other responsible person** — s.43C (lowest threshold)
- **Legal adviser** in the course of obtaining legal advice — s.43D
- **Minister of the Crown** (where employer is appointed by statute) — s.43E
- **Prescribed person** (e.g., FCA, CQC, HSE) — s.43F (must reasonably believe information is substantially true)
- **Wider disclosure** (e.g., media) — s.43G/43H (highest threshold: must not be for personal gain, must be reasonable in all circumstances, and must satisfy additional conditions)

### Protections

- **Detriment**: A worker has the right not to be subjected to any detriment by any act, or deliberate failure to act, by the employer on the ground that the worker has made a protected disclosure (s.47B ERA 1996).
- **Dismissal**: Dismissal for making a protected disclosure is **automatically unfair** (s.103A ERA 1996) — no qualifying service period and **compensation is uncapped**.
- **Interim relief**: Available where the employee claims automatically unfair dismissal for whistleblowing — application must be made within **7 days** of the effective date of termination (s.128 ERA 1996).
