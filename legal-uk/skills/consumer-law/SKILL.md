## 1. Consumer Rights Act 2015 — Overview

The CRA 2015 consolidated and replaced the Sale of Goods Act 1979, Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 (for B2C), Unfair Terms in Consumer Contracts Regulations 1999, and the Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982 (for B2C), in relation to consumer contracts. It applies where:
- A **trader** (s.2(2)) — a person acting for purposes relating to their trade, business, craft, or profession.
- Supplies goods, digital content, or services to a **consumer** (s.2(3)) — an individual acting for purposes wholly or mainly outside their trade, business, craft, or profession.

## 2. Chapter 2 — Goods (ss.3-18 CRA 2015)

### Statutory Rights

| Right | Section | Description |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Satisfactory quality | s.9 | Goods must meet the standard that a reasonable person would consider satisfactory, considering description, price, and all other relevant circumstances. Includes fitness for common purposes, appearance/finish, freedom from minor defects, safety, durability. |
| Fitness for particular purpose | s.10 | Where the consumer makes known a particular purpose (expressly or by implication) and the trader supplies goods in the course of business, goods must be reasonably fit for that purpose. |
| As described | s.11 | Goods must match any description given by or on behalf of the trader. If sale is by sample and description, bulk must correspond with both. |
| Match sample | s.13 | Goods must match a sample shown, in quality and any features that make the sample satisfactory. |
| Match model | s.14 | Goods must match a model seen or examined, except for differences brought to consumer's attention. |
| Right to supply | s.17 | Trader must have the right to sell goods; goods must be free from charges/encumbrances not disclosed; consumer must enjoy quiet possession. |
| Installation | s.15 | If installation is part of the contract and done incorrectly, treated as breach of goods quality. |
| Digital content in goods | s.16 | Where goods include digital content, the digital content must conform to the digital content chapter (Chapter 3). |

### Remedies Hierarchy for Goods

#### Tier 1 — Short-Term Right to Reject (s.20)

- Available within **30 days** of delivery/ownership/completion of contract (whichever is later).
- Consumer entitled to a **full refund** (s.20(10)).
- The 30-day period does not run during any period where the consumer has requested or agreed to a repair or replacement (s.20(9)).
- Perishable goods: shorter period as reasonable in the circumstances (s.22(3)).

#### Tier 2 — Repair or Replacement (s.23)

- After the 30-day short-term rejection period, the consumer may request **repair or replacement**.
- Trader must do so within a **reasonable time** and without **significant inconvenience** to the consumer (s.23(2)(a)-(b)).
- Trader bears the cost, including carriage, labour, and materials (s.23(2)(c)).
- Consumer cannot require a remedy that is impossible or disproportionate compared to the other remedy (s.23(3)).
- One repair or replacement is the default threshold for triggering Tier 3 rights.

#### Tier 3 — Price Reduction or Final Right to Reject (s.24)

Available if:
- Repair or replacement is impossible (s.24(5)(a)).
- Trader fails to repair/replace within reasonable time or without significant inconvenience (s.24(5)(b)).
- Repair/replacement has been carried out once and the goods still do not conform (s.24(5)(c)).

**Price reduction**: appropriate amount reflecting the difference between conforming and non-conforming goods (s.24(1)).

**Final right to reject**: consumer returns goods and receives a refund. The trader may make a **deduction for use** unless the rejection is within **6 months** of delivery (s.24(10)) — no deduction in the first 6 months except for motor vehicles (s.24(11)).

### Burden of Proof (s.19(14)-(15))

- Within the first **6 months**: any lack of conformity is **presumed** to have existed at the time of delivery, unless the trader proves otherwise or the presumption is incompatible with the nature of the goods or non-conformity.
- After 6 months: consumer bears the burden of proving the defect existed at or before delivery.

## 3. Chapter 3 — Digital Content (ss.33-47 CRA 2015)

### What is Digital Content? (s.33(1))

Data produced and supplied in digital form. Examples: software, apps, music downloads, e-books, films, games, firmware updates.

### Statutory Rights

| Right | Section | Description |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Satisfactory quality | s.34 | Same test as goods; adapted for digital context |
| Fitness for particular purpose | s.35 | Same principle as goods |
| As described | s.36 | Must match description |
| Right to supply | s.41 | Trader must have the right to supply |
| No device damage | **s.46** | Where digital content causes damage to a device or other digital content belonging to the consumer, the trader must compensate if they failed to use reasonable care and skill. Applies even to free digital content supplied alongside paid content. |

### Remedies for Digital Content (ss.42-46)

| Remedy | Section | Detail |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Repair or replacement | s.43 | First-line remedy; reasonable time, no significant inconvenience, at trader's cost |
| Price reduction | s.44 | If repair/replacement impossible, not done in reasonable time, or done once and still non-conforming |
| Refund | s.45 | Where price reduction is the available remedy, the consumer may reject and obtain a refund |
| Compensation for device damage | s.46 | Separate right regardless of conformity rights |

**No short-term right to reject** for digital content (unlike goods). The consumer moves directly to repair/replacement, then price reduction/refund.

### Updates and Modifications

- If the contract provides that updates will be supplied, they must be supplied for a reasonable period or as specified (s.40).
- Modifications must not adversely affect the consumer's ability to use the digital content (an implied term by analogy with case law, though not expressly stated in s.40).

## 4. Chapter 4 — Services (ss.48-57 CRA 2015)

### Statutory Rights

| Right | Section | Description |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Reasonable care and skill | s.49 | Service must be performed with reasonable care and skill (non-excludable — s.57(1)) |
| Information binding | s.50 | Where the trader gives the consumer information (orally or in writing) about the service or trader, and the consumer relies on it, it is treated as a term of the contract. Excludable by clear notice. |
| Reasonable price | s.51 | Where price is not determined or determinable, consumer pays a reasonable price |
| Reasonable time | s.52 | Where time for performance is not fixed, service must be performed within a reasonable time |

### Remedies for Services (ss.54-56)

| Remedy | Section | Detail |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Repeat performance | s.55 | Trader must redo the service to conform, within reasonable time, at no extra cost, without significant inconvenience |
| Price reduction | s.56 | If repeat performance is impossible or not done within reasonable time — appropriate reduction, which may be up to 100% (full refund) |

**No right to reject** for services — only repeat performance and price reduction.

## 5. Part 2 — Unfair Terms (ss.61-76 CRA 2015)

### The Fairness Test (s.62)

A term in a consumer contract is **unfair** if, contrary to the requirement of good faith, it causes a **significant imbalance** in the parties' rights and obligations to the **detriment of the consumer** (s.62(4)-(5)).

### Core Terms Exemption (s.64)

- The fairness assessment does not apply to a term that specifies the **main subject matter** of the contract or the **assessment of the price** payable — BUT only if:
  - The term is **transparent** (expressed in plain and intelligible language, and legible — s.64(3)-(4)); AND
  - The term is **prominent** (brought to the consumer's attention in such a way that an average consumer would be aware of it — s.64(4)).
- If a core term is not transparent and prominent, it IS subject to the fairness test.

### Effect of Unfairness (s.67)

An unfair term is **not binding on the consumer**. The contract continues in force so far as practicable without the unfair term.

### Grey List (Schedule 2 CRA 2015)

Schedule 2 sets out an indicative (non-exhaustive) list of terms that may be regarded as unfair. Key examples:

| Para | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Excluding/limiting trader's liability for death/personal injury (always unfair — s.65) |
| 2 | Inappropriately excluding legal rights |
| 4 | Permitting trader to retain pre-payments where trader cancels |
| 5 | Requiring consumer to pay disproportionate compensation for breach |
| 6 | Allowing trader to dissolve contract on a discretionary basis where same not available to consumer |
| 9 | Binding the consumer to terms they had no real opportunity to become acquainted with before contract |
| 11 | Enabling trader to alter terms unilaterally |
| 12 | Enabling trader to alter characteristics of goods/services without valid reason |
| 14 | Enabling trader to increase price without giving consumer right to cancel |
| 16 | Limiting trader's obligation to respect commitments undertaken by agents |
| 20 | Terms requiring disputes to be referred exclusively to an arbitration scheme not covered by legal provisions |

### Terms That Are Always Unfair (s.65)

- A term that purports to exclude or restrict the trader's liability for **death or personal injury** resulting from negligence is always unfair and not binding (s.65(1)).
- This reflects the Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 s.2(1) position.

## 6. Consumer Contracts (Information, Cancellation and Additional Charges) Regulations 2013 (SI 2013/3134) — "CCR 2013"

### Scope

Applies to **distance contracts** (online, phone, mail order) and **off-premises contracts** (concluded away from trader's premises or where consumer was solicited in person away from premises) between a trader and consumer.

### 14-Day Cooling-Off Period (Reg.29-38)

- Consumer has the right to cancel the contract within **14 days** without giving any reason and without incurring any liability (except return costs — reg.35(5)).
- The cancellation period begins:
  - **Goods**: from the day after delivery (reg.30(2)-(5)).
  - **Services/digital content**: from the day after the contract is entered into (reg.30(1)).
- Trader must provide a model cancellation form (Sch.3) and clear information about the right to cancel.
- If the trader fails to inform the consumer of the right to cancel, the cancellation period extends by up to **12 months** (reg.31(2)).

### Exceptions to the Right to Cancel (Reg.28(1))

The 14-day cooling-off right does NOT apply to:

| Exception | Reg.28(1) |
|-----------|-----------|
| **(a)** Services fully performed with consumer's prior express consent and acknowledgment that cancellation right will be lost | Para (a) |
| **(b)** Goods/services where price depends on financial market fluctuations beyond the trader's control | Para (b) |
| **(c)** Goods made to the consumer's specifications or clearly personalised | Para (c) |
| **(d)** Goods liable to deteriorate or expire rapidly (perishable) | Para (d) |
| **(e)** Sealed goods unsuitable for return due to health protection or hygiene, if unsealed after delivery | Para (e) |
| **(f)** Goods inseparably mixed with other items after delivery | Para (f) |
| **(g)** Alcoholic beverages where price agreed at contract but delivery after 30 days, price dependent on market fluctuations | Para (g) |
| **(h)** Urgent repair/maintenance where consumer specifically requested a visit | Para (h) |
| **(i)** Sealed audio, video, or software recordings if unsealed | Para (i) |
| **(j)** Newspapers, periodicals, magazines (except subscription contracts) | Para (j) |
| **(k)** Contracts concluded at public auction | Para (k) |
| **(l)** Accommodation, transport, catering, leisure services for a specific date/period | Para (l) |
| **(m)** Digital content not supplied on a tangible medium, if performance begun with consumer's express consent and acknowledgment of loss of cancellation right | Para (m) |

### Refund on Cancellation (Reg.34)

- Trader must reimburse all payments received (including outward delivery costs at basic rate) within **14 days** of receiving the returned goods or evidence of return (reg.34(4)-(5)).
- Consumer pays return costs unless the trader has agreed to bear them or failed to inform the consumer they must pay (reg.35(5)).

## 7. Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 (SI 2008/1277) — "CPRs 2008"

### General Prohibitions

| Prohibition | Regulation |
|-------------|-----------|
| Unfair commercial practices | Reg.3 — practice is unfair if it contravenes professional diligence and materially distorts consumer's economic behaviour |
| Misleading actions | Reg.5 — false information or deceptive overall presentation about products, price, trader's nature |
| Misleading omissions | Reg.6 — omitting/hiding material information or providing it in unclear/untimely manner |
| Aggressive practices | Reg.7 — harassment, coercion, or undue influence impairing consumer's freedom of choice |

### 31 Blacklisted Practices (Schedule 1)

These are practices considered unfair in all circumstances (no need to prove impact on consumer behaviour):

| # | Practice |
|---|---------|
| 1 | Claiming to be a signatory of a code of conduct when not |
| 2 | Displaying a trust mark without authorisation |
| 5 | Bait advertising (advertising products at a specified price without reasonable stock) |
| 6 | Bait and switch (advertising at low price then refusing to show/supply it to promote different product) |
| 7 | Falsely stating product only available for a limited time |
| 10 | Presenting rights given by law as a distinctive feature of the trader's offer |
| 11 | Using editorial content in media to promote a product where the trader has paid for it, without being clear (advertorial) |
| 12 | Making materially inaccurate claims about risk to consumer's safety |
| 15 | Claiming the trader is about to cease trading or relocate when not |
| 17 | Falsely claiming a product can cure illness, dysfunction, or malformation |
| 20 | Describing a product as "free" when the consumer must pay anything other than the unavoidable cost of responding/collecting |
| 22 | Including an invoice/demand for payment for unsolicited products |
| 24 | Creating the false impression that after-sales service is available in another Member State |
| 25 | Requiring a consumer seeking to claim on insurance to produce documents not relevant to the claim |
| 28 | Including in an advertisement a direct exhortation to children to buy or persuade parents to buy |
| 29 | Demanding immediate or deferred payment for products supplied by the trader but not solicited (inertia selling) |
| 31 | Creating the false impression that the consumer has won a prize |

### Consumer Right of Redress (Part 4A, regs.27A-27L)

Consumers have a direct private right of action against traders for misleading actions (reg.5) and aggressive practices (reg.7):
- **Right to unwind** the contract within 90 days (reg.27E).
- **Right to a discount** (25%, 50%, 75%, or 100% depending on severity) if unwinding not available (reg.27I).
- **Right to damages** for consequential loss where trader knew or ought to have known (reg.27J).

## 8. DMCC Act 2024 — Subscription Contracts and Fake Reviews (from 6 April 2025)

### Subscription Contracts (DMCC 2024 Part 4, Sch.20)

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Pre-contract information | Trader must give full, clear, and timely information about the subscription, including: total price, billing frequency, how and when to cancel, renewal terms |
| Cooling-off period | Consumer has a **14-day cooling-off period** for new subscriptions and renewals |
| Renewal reminders | Trader must send a **reminder notice** before each renewal period, clearly stating: renewal date, price, how to cancel, that the contract will auto-renew unless cancelled |
| Easy cancellation | Cancellation must be possible through a **straightforward, accessible, and timely process** — must not be more difficult than sign-up; no excessive friction, no requirement to call at specific times, no dark patterns |
| Consequences of non-compliance | Contract may be unenforceable; CMA may impose penalties up to 10% of global turnover |

### Fake Reviews (DMCC 2024 Part 4, Sch.19 amending CPRs 2008 Schedule 1)

New blacklisted practices added from 6 April 2025:

| Practice | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Commissioning fake reviews | Paying or incentivising someone to write a review that does not reflect a genuine experience (para 31A) |
| Publishing/facilitating fake reviews | Knowingly publishing or hosting fake reviews or failing to take reasonable steps to remove them (para 31B) |
| Offering to submit fake reviews | Advertising or offering services to write, submit, or facilitate fake reviews (para 31C) |
| Suppressing genuine negative reviews | Selectively publishing only positive reviews or systematically suppressing negative ones |

### Drip Pricing (DMCC 2024 Sch.19 para 6A)

- Traders must include all mandatory and unavoidable fees in the headline price displayed to consumers.
- Charges revealed only at checkout or later in the purchasing journey, which the consumer cannot avoid, constitute an unfair commercial practice.
- Optional extras (e.g., insurance, priority services) may be offered separately if genuinely optional and not pre-selected.

## 9. Enforcement Landscape

### Key Enforcers

| Body | Powers |
|------|--------|
| CMA | Direct enforcement from 6 April 2025 (DMCC Part 3); penalties up to 10% global turnover; undertakings; enhanced consumer measures |
| Local authority Trading Standards | Enforcement of CPRs 2008, CRA 2015, CCR 2013; powers under Enterprise Act 2002 Part 8 (enhanced orders from 6 April 2025) |
| Sector regulators (FCA, Ofcom, etc.) | Concurrent consumer enforcement powers |

### Consumer Redress Routes

| Route | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Direct complaint to trader | Always the first step |
| Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) | Trader must inform consumer of certified ADR entity (Alternative Dispute Resolution for Consumer Disputes (Competent Authorities and Information) Regulations 2015 SI 2015/542) |
| County Court claim (Small Claims Track) | Claims up to £10,000 (England/Wales); simplified procedure; no costs risk |
| CMA/Trading Standards complaint | Consumer reports; may trigger enforcement action |
| Section 75 Consumer Credit Act 1974 | If paid by credit card (£100-£30,000), equal liability of card issuer and trader |
| Chargeback (card scheme rules) | Informal route via Visa/Mastercard rules; time-limited |

## 10. Practical Guidance for SMBs

### Compliance Checklist for Online Sellers

1. **Pre-contract information** (CCR 2013 Sch.2): provide the 14 required items including main characteristics of goods/services, total price (inclusive of taxes), delivery costs, right to cancel, cancellation form.
2. **Cancellation rights**: provide model cancellation form; honour 14-day cooling-off; process refunds within 14 days of receiving returned goods.
3. **Terms and conditions**: review against CRA 2015 Part 2 unfair terms provisions; ensure core terms are transparent and prominent.
4. **Product descriptions**: accurate, not misleading (CRA 2015 s.11; CPRs 2008 reg.5).
5. **Reviews**: do not suppress negative reviews; do not commission or incentivise fake reviews (DMCC 2024 from 6 April 2025).
6. **Subscriptions**: provide clear renewal notices; make cancellation as easy as sign-up (DMCC 2024 from 6 April 2025).
7. **Pricing**: include all unavoidable charges in the headline price; no drip pricing (DMCC 2024 from 6 April 2025).
8. **Complaints**: have a clear complaints procedure; respond within 14 days (best practice).
9. **Returns**: clearly state returns policy; for faulty goods, remember the CRA 2015 remedy hierarchy (30-day reject, repair/replace, price reduction/final reject).
10. **Record-keeping**: keep evidence of pre-contract information provided, cancellation requests, refund processing.
