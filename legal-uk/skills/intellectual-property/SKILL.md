## 1. Copyright (Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988)

### Automatic Protection

Copyright arises automatically upon creation of an original work that is recorded in material form (s.1(1) CDPA 1988). No registration required in the UK. The originality threshold is the author's "own intellectual creation" (Infopaq International A/S v Danske Dagblades Forening [2009] ECDR 16).

### Categories of Work (s.1(1))

| Category | Examples | Section |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Literary works | Novels, code, databases, tables, compilations | s.3 |
| Dramatic works | Plays, screenplays, choreography | s.3(1) |
| Musical works | Musical compositions (not lyrics — those are literary) | s.3(1) |
| Artistic works | Paintings, photographs, sculptures, architecture, graphic works | s.4 |
| Sound recordings | Any recording of sounds from which sounds may be reproduced | s.5A |
| Films | Any recording from which a moving image may be produced | s.5B |
| Broadcasts | Electronic transmission for simultaneous reception by public | s.6 |
| Typographical arrangements | Published layout of literary, dramatic, or musical work | s.8 |

### Duration of Copyright

| Work Type | Duration | Section |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| Literary, dramatic, musical, artistic | Life of author + 70 years | s.12(2) |
| Sound recordings | 70 years from publication (extended from 50 by SI 2013/1782) | s.13A |
| Films | 70 years from death of last to die of: principal director, author of screenplay, author of dialogue, composer of specifically created music | s.13B |
| Broadcasts | 50 years from first broadcast | s.14 |
| Typographical arrangements | 25 years from first publication | s.15 |
| Crown copyright | 125 years from creation, or 50 from publication if published in first 75 years | s.163 |
| Parliamentary copyright | 50 years from making | s.165 |

### Ownership (s.11 CDPA 1988)

- **General rule**: first owner is the author (s.11(1)).
- **Employment exception**: where a literary, dramatic, musical, or artistic work, or a film, is made by an employee in the course of employment, the employer is the first owner (s.11(2)), subject to any agreement to the contrary.
- **Commissioned works**: the commissioner does NOT automatically own copyright (contrast with the pre-1988 position for certain works). Ownership must be assigned in writing (s.90(3)).

### Moral Rights (CDPA 1988 Chapter IV)

| Right | Section | Duration | Waivable? |
|-------|---------|----------|-----------|
| Right to be identified as author/director (paternity right) | s.77 | Same as copyright | Yes (s.87) |
| Right to object to derogatory treatment (integrity right) | s.80 | Same as copyright | Yes (s.87) |
| Right not to have work falsely attributed | s.84 | 20 years from death | N/A |
| Right to privacy of photographs/films commissioned for private purposes | s.85 | Same as copyright | Yes (s.87) |

The paternity right must be asserted (s.78) — typically done in publishing contracts or on a work itself.

### Database Rights (Copyright and Rights in Databases Regulations 1997, SI 1997/3032)

- Sui generis right, separate from copyright.
- Protects databases where there has been "substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting the contents" (reg.13).
- Duration: 15 years from completion; re-starts on substantial new investment (reg.17).
- Post-Brexit: UK retains database right for UK nationals/residents and UK-incorporated companies. EU database right no longer automatically extends to UK makers.

## 2. Trade Marks (Trade Marks Act 1994)

### Registration

- Apply to the UK Intellectual Property Office (UKIPO).
- Application fee: £170 for one class + £50 per additional class (online).
- Nice Classification: 45 classes (34 goods, 11 services).
- Examination: absolute grounds (s.3 TMA 1994) and relative grounds (s.5) assessed.

### Absolute Grounds for Refusal (s.3 TMA 1994)

- Signs that are not capable of being represented graphically or distinguishing goods/services (s.3(1)(a)-(d)).
- Descriptive marks, customary marks, shapes resulting from nature of goods/adding substantial value.
- Marks contrary to public policy or morality (s.3(3)(a)).
- Deceptive marks (s.3(3)(b)).
- Bad faith applications (s.3(6)).

### Relative Grounds for Refusal (s.5 TMA 1994)

- Identical mark / identical goods or services (s.5(1)).
- Identical or similar mark / identical or similar goods where likelihood of confusion (s.5(2)).
- Mark with reputation — unfair advantage or detriment (s.5(3)).

### Duration and Renewal

- Initial registration: 10 years from filing date (s.42(1)).
- Renewable indefinitely for further 10-year periods (s.43(1)).
- Renewal fee: £200 for one class + £50 per additional class (online).
- Non-use: vulnerable to revocation if not put to genuine use within 5 years of registration (s.46(1)(a)-(b)).

### Madrid Protocol

- UK businesses can use a UK trade mark application/registration as a basis for an international application under the Madrid Protocol (administered by WIPO).
- Designate individual countries or the EU (via EUIPO).
- Post-Brexit: existing EU trade marks that covered the UK were automatically cloned as comparable UK trade marks (SI 2019/269).

### Infringement (s.10 TMA 1994)

| Type | Section | Test |
|------|---------|------|
| Identical mark / identical goods | s.10(1) | Per se infringement |
| Identical/similar mark / identical/similar goods | s.10(2) | Likelihood of confusion |
| Mark with reputation | s.10(3) | Unfair advantage or detriment, without due cause |
| Use on packaging, advertising, business papers | s.10(4) | Any of the above three types |

### Passing Off (Common Law)

Even without registration, a business can protect goodwill through the tort of passing off. Elements (Reckitt & Colman Products Ltd v Borden Inc [1990] 1 WLR 491 — the "Jif Lemon" case):
1. Goodwill attached to the claimant's goods/services.
2. Misrepresentation by the defendant.
3. Damage (or likelihood of damage) to the claimant.

## 3. Patents (Patents Act 1977)

### Patentability Requirements (s.1 PA 1977)

An invention is patentable if:
1. It is **new** (novelty — s.2).
2. It involves an **inventive step** (non-obvious to a person skilled in the art — s.3).
3. It is capable of **industrial application** (s.4).
4. It is not excluded subject matter (s.1(2)-(3)).

### Excluded Subject Matter (s.1(2))

- Discoveries, scientific theories, mathematical methods.
- Literary, dramatic, musical, or artistic works (copyright protects these).
- Schemes, rules, or methods for performing a mental act, playing a game, doing business.
- Programs for computers (but see below).
- Presentation of information.

Exclusion applies only to the extent that the application relates to the thing "as such" (s.1(2)).

### Software Patentability — The Aerotel/Macrossan Test

The Court of Appeal in Aerotel Ltd v Telco Holdings Ltd [2006] EWCA Civ 1371 established a four-step test:
1. Properly construe the claim.
2. Identify the actual contribution.
3. Ask whether the contribution falls solely within excluded matter.
4. Check whether the actual or alleged contribution is actually technical.

A computer program that produces a technical effect beyond the normal physical interaction between program and computer may be patentable (AT&T Knowledge Ventures [2009] EWHC 343 (Pat) — five "signposts").

### Duration and Fees

- Maximum term: 20 years from filing date (s.25(1)).
- Renewal fees: payable annually from 5th year (s.25(3); Sch.4A).
- Filing fee: £310 combined (application £30 + search £150 + examination £130).
- Supplementary Protection Certificates (SPCs): extend protection for pharmaceuticals/plant protection products up to 5 years beyond patent expiry.

### Employee Inventions (ss.39-43 PA 1977)

- An invention belongs to the **employer** if (s.39(1)):
  - (a) Made in the course of normal duties or specifically assigned duties, where an invention might reasonably be expected to result from carrying out those duties; OR
  - (b) Made in the course of the employee's duties and the employee had a special obligation to further the interests of the employer's undertaking by the nature of those duties and particular responsibilities.
- All other employee inventions belong to the **employee** (s.39(2)).
- **Compensation** (s.40): an employee may claim compensation if the invention is of outstanding benefit to the employer and it is just that compensation be awarded. Threshold is very high (Kelly v GE Healthcare Ltd [2009] RPC 12).
- Any contract term diminishing employee rights under ss.39-42 is unenforceable (s.42(2)).

## 4. Designs

### Overview of UK Design Rights Post-Brexit

| Right | Duration | Registration? | Legislation |
|-------|----------|--------------|-------------|
| UK Unregistered Design Right (UDR) | 15 years from creation (or 10 from first marketing, if earlier); last 5 years subject to licences of right | No | CDPA 1988 Part III (ss.213-264) |
| Supplementary Unregistered Design (SUD) | 3 years from first disclosure in UK | No | SI 2019/638, replacing former EU UDR |
| UK Registered Design | 5 years from registration, renewable up to max 25 years | Yes (UKIPO) | Registered Designs Act 1949 (as amended) |

### UK Unregistered Design Right (CDPA 1988 ss.213-264)

- Protects the shape or configuration (internal or external) of the whole or part of an article (s.213(2)).
- Does NOT protect surface decoration, methods or principles of construction, or "must fit"/"must match" features (s.213(3)).
- Qualification: designer or commissioner must be a qualifying person (UK/EU/qualifying country national or company — s.217-220).
- Infringement: making articles exactly or substantially to the design (s.226).

### Supplementary Unregistered Design (SUD)

- Created by The Design Right (Supplementary Unregistered Design) Regulations 2019 (SI 2019/638) to replace the former EU unregistered Community design right in the UK.
- Protects the appearance (lines, contours, colours, shape, texture, materials, ornamentation) of the whole or part of a product.
- Duration: 3 years from the date on which the design was first made available to the public in the UK.
- Must be new and have individual character (same tests as registered designs).

### UK Registered Design (Registered Designs Act 1949)

- Protects the appearance of the whole or part of a product resulting from features of lines, contours, colours, shape, texture, materials, and/or ornamentation (s.1(2) RDA 1949).
- Requirements: novelty (s.1B(2)) and individual character (s.1B(3)) — informed user overall impression test.
- Duration: 5-year initial period, renewable in 5-year increments to maximum 25 years (s.8).
- Registration fee: £50 for one design (online); £70 paper.
- Grace period: 12 months from first disclosure by designer (s.1B(6)(d)) — allows testing the market before filing.
- Infringement: use of a design that does not produce a different overall impression on the informed user (s.7(1)).

## 5. Employee IP — Summary Table

| IP Right | Default Ownership Rule | Statute |
|----------|----------------------|---------|
| Copyright | Employer owns if made by employee in course of employment | s.11(2) CDPA 1988 |
| Patents | Employer owns if made in course of normal/assigned duties where invention expected, or special obligation | s.39(1) PA 1977 |
| Trade marks | No statutory rule; employer typically owns marks used in business | Common law / contract |
| Designs (UDR) | Commissioner owns if commissioned; employer owns if made in course of employment | s.215 CDPA 1988 |
| Registered designs | First owner is the creator unless made under commission or employment | s.2(1A)-(1B) RDA 1949 |
| Confidential information | Employer protected by implied duty of fidelity during employment; post-termination limited to trade secrets | Faccenda Chicken Ltd v Fowler [1987] Ch 117 |

**Practical note for SMBs**: Always include an IP assignment clause in employment contracts and contractor agreements. For contractors (who are not employees), neither s.11(2) CDPA 1988 nor s.39 PA 1977 applies — a written assignment is essential.

### Available Remedies Across IP Rights

| Remedy | Copyright (s.96-100 CDPA) | Trade Mark (s.14-19 TMA) | Patent (s.61-62 PA) | Design (s.24A-24G RDA) |

| Damages OR account of profits | Yes (s.96(2)) | Yes (s.14(2)) | Yes (s.61(1)(c)-(d)) | Yes (s.24A) |

| Additional damages | Yes (s.97(2)) | No | No | No |

### Limitation Periods

- All IP infringement claims: 6 years from accrual of cause of action (Limitation Act 1980 s.2 for tort).
- Each infringing act starts a fresh limitation period.

## 7. IP Licensing Essentials

### Key Terms in an IP Licence

1. **Grant clause**: exclusive, sole, or non-exclusive.
   - Exclusive: only the licensee may use (even the licensor is excluded).
   - Sole: licensee and licensor may use, but no further licences granted.
   - Non-exclusive: licensor may grant further licences.
2. **Scope**: territory, field of use, channels, sublicensing rights.
3. **Duration**: fixed term, perpetual, or linked to IP duration.
4. **Royalties/fees**: percentage of net sales, lump sum, minimum guarantees.
5. **Quality control** (especially trade marks — naked licensing risks abandonment).
6. **Assignment of licence**: typically requires licensor consent.
7. **Termination**: breach, insolvency, change of control.

### Formalities

- Copyright licence: no formality required, but exclusive licences must be in writing and signed by the licensor to confer standing to sue (s.92(1) CDPA 1988).
- Patent licence: no formality required; registration at UKIPO advisable (s.33 PA 1977 — unregistered transactions void against subsequent purchaser for value).
- Trade mark licence: must be in writing and signed (s.28(2) TMA 1994); register at UKIPO.
- Registered design licence: register at UKIPO (s.19 RDA 1949).

## 8. Post-Brexit IP Considerations

| Issue | Position |
|-------|----------|
| EU trade marks | No longer cover the UK; existing EUTMs cloned as comparable UK marks (SI 2019/269) |
| Registered Community Designs | Cloned as re-registered UK designs (SI 2019/638) |
| EU Unregistered Community Design | No longer arises for UK disclosures; replaced by SUD |
| Exhaustion of rights | UK adopted "UK+" exhaustion — goods put on market in UK or EEA exhaust UK rights; EEA does not reciprocate |
| Copyright | Largely unaffected (Berne Convention based) |
| Patent | Unaffected (European Patent Convention is not an EU instrument) |
| Unified Patent Court | UK withdrew; UPC decisions do not bind UK courts |
| SPC | UK maintains own SPC regime; EU SPCs no longer extend to UK |
