## 1. Company Formation

### Types of Company (Companies Act 2006 Part 1)

| Type | Section | Key Feature |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Private limited by shares (Ltd) | s.3(1) | Most common SMB vehicle; no minimum share capital |
| Private limited by guarantee | s.3(3) | Common for non-profits; members guarantee a nominal sum |
| Public limited company (PLC) | s.4(2) | Minimum allotted share capital £50,000 (s.763); may offer shares to public |
| Community Interest Company (CIC) | CIC Regulations 2005 | Asset lock; community benefit test |
| Unlimited company | s.3(4) | No limit on member liability; rare |

### Formation Requirements (ss.9-16 CA 2006)

- **Memorandum of association** (s.8) — subscribers' names and agreement to become members.
- **Application for registration** (s.9) — Form IN01, delivered to Companies House with:
  - Proposed articles of association (or adopt Model Articles SI 2008/3229).
  - Statement of capital and initial shareholdings (s.10).
  - Statement of proposed officers (s.12).
  - Statement of compliance (s.13).
  - Registered office address (s.9(5)(a)).
- **Certificate of incorporation** (s.15) — conclusive evidence company is formed.
- **Filing fee** — standard £50 online / £71 paper; same-day £30 online / £100 paper.

### Model Articles (SI 2008/3229)

Default articles apply unless displaced or supplemented by bespoke articles (s.20 CA 2006). Three sets:
1. Private companies limited by shares (Schedule 1).
2. Private companies limited by guarantee (Schedule 2).
3. Public companies (Schedule 3).

Key Model Article provisions (private Ltd by shares):
- Directors may delegate (Art 5).
- Directors' general authority (Art 3) — subject to articles, directors manage.
- Calling general meetings (Art 37).
- No share transfer restrictions by default (contrast with common bespoke pre-emption clauses).

## 2. Directors' Duties (ss.171-177 Companies Act 2006)

These are codified fiduciary duties owed to the company (s.170(1)). They replaced the prior common law and equitable duties, but case law decided under the old rules remains relevant for interpretation (s.170(4)).

### s.171 — Duty to Act Within Powers

Directors must act in accordance with the company's constitution (articles, shareholder resolutions) and only exercise powers for the purposes for which they are conferred. Misuse of power for an improper purpose is a breach even if the director believed it benefited the company (Howard Smith Ltd v Ampol Petroleum Ltd [1974] AC 821).

### s.172 — Duty to Promote the Success of the Company

The director must act in a way they consider, in good faith, would most likely promote the success of the company for the benefit of its members as a whole, having regard to:
- (a) Likely consequences of any decision in the long term.
- (b) Interests of the company's employees.
- (c) Need to foster business relationships with suppliers, customers, others.
- (d) Impact on the community and the environment.
- (e) Desirability of maintaining a reputation for high standards of business conduct.
- (f) Need to act fairly as between members.

This is the "enlightened shareholder value" approach. Where the company is insolvent or nearing insolvency, the duty shifts to creditors' interests (s.172(3), confirmed in BTI 2014 LLC v Sequana SA [2022] UKSC 25).

### s.173 — Duty to Exercise Independent Judgment

Directors must not fetter their discretion, e.g., by blindly following the instructions of a shareholder or third party. However, acting in accordance with an agreement entered into by the company or the company's constitution is permitted (s.173(2)).

### s.174 — Duty to Exercise Reasonable Care, Skill and Diligence

Measured by a dual objective/subjective test:
- **Objective limb**: the general knowledge, skill, and experience reasonably expected of a person carrying out the functions of that director.
- **Subjective limb**: the general knowledge, skill, and experience that the particular director actually has.

The higher standard applies (Re D'Jan of London Ltd [1994] 1 BCLC 561).

### s.175 — Duty to Avoid Conflicts of Interest

Applies to the exploitation of any property, information, or opportunity, whether or not the company could take advantage of it. Does not apply to transactions or arrangements with the company (those fall under s.177).

Authorisation: In private companies, other directors may authorise the conflict unless the articles prevent this (s.175(5)(a)). In public companies, the articles must expressly permit authorisation.

### s.176 — Duty Not to Accept Benefits from Third Parties

A director must not accept a benefit from a third party conferred by reason of being a director or doing (or not doing) anything as director. No breach where the benefit cannot reasonably be regarded as likely to give rise to a conflict.

### s.177 — Duty to Declare Interest in Proposed Transaction

Before the company enters a transaction or arrangement in which a director has a direct or indirect interest, the director must declare the nature and extent of that interest to the other directors (s.177(1)). Declaration may be at a board meeting, by written notice (s.184), or by general notice (s.185). Failure to declare is a criminal offence (s.183).

For existing transactions, s.182 imposes a separate obligation to declare.

## 3. Small and Micro-Entity Thresholds

### Small Company (s.382 CA 2006, as amended)

A company qualifies as small if it meets at least two of three criteria in the current and preceding financial year:

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Turnover | Not more than £10.2 million |
| Balance sheet total | Not more than £5.1 million |
| Average number of employees | Not more than 50 |

Small company exemptions:
- Abbreviated accounts no longer permitted (from 2016), but may file filleted accounts (omit P&L and directors' report at Companies House).
- Audit exemption (s.477) — unless the company is part of a group failing the small group test, a public company, or certain regulated entities.

### Micro-Entity (s.384A CA 2006, SI 2013/3008)

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Turnover | Not more than £632,000 |
| Balance sheet total | Not more than £316,000 |
| Average number of employees | Not more than 10 |

Micro-entity exemptions:
- Prepare micro-entity minimum accounts (abridged balance sheet, limited notes).
- No requirement to prepare a directors' report or P&L for filing.
- No requirement to include a true and fair view override statement (s.393(1A)).

## 4. Annual Accounts and Filing Obligations

### Filing Deadlines

| Company Type | Deadline After Year-End | Section |
|--------------|------------------------|---------|
| Private company | 9 months | s.442(2)(a) |
| Public company | 6 months | s.442(2)(b) |
| First accounts (private) | 21 months from incorporation or 9 months from year-end, whichever is longer | s.442(3) |

### Late Filing Penalty Table (s.453, SI 2008/497 as amended)

| Duration Late | Private Company | Public Company |
|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| Up to 1 month | £150 | £750 |
| 1-3 months | £375 | £1,500 |
| 3-6 months | £750 | £3,000 |
| Over 6 months | £1,500 | £7,500 |

Penalties double if accounts are filed late in two successive financial years (from 10 August 2024, SI 2024/644).

### Confirmation Statement (s.853A CA 2006)

- Must be filed at least once every 12 months.
- Confirms company details are up to date (registered office, directors, PSCs, SIC codes, share capital).
- Filing fee: £34 (online) / £62 (paper).
- Failure is a criminal offence (s.853L).

## 5. PSC Register (Part 21A CA 2006, ss.790A-790ZG)

A Person with Significant Control is an individual who:
- Holds more than 25% of shares (s.790B(2)(a)).
- Holds more than 25% of voting rights (s.790B(2)(b)).
- Holds the right to appoint or remove a majority of directors (s.790B(2)(c)).
- Has the right to exercise, or actually exercises, significant influence or control (s.790B(2)(d)-(e)).

Companies must:
- Maintain a PSC register (s.790M) or elect to keep information on the central register at Companies House (s.790X).
- Take reasonable steps to identify PSCs (s.790D).
- Deliver PSC information with confirmation statements.
- Update within 14 days of change; notify Companies House within a further 14 days.

Failure to comply: criminal offence (s.790R) — unlimited fine on conviction on indictment.

## 6. ECCTA 2023 (Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023)

### Identity Verification

- All new and existing directors, PSCs, and those filing on behalf of companies must verify their identity with Companies House (ECCTA 2023 ss.48-65, inserting new Part 4A CA 2006).
- Verification may be direct (via Companies House) or indirect (via an Authorised Corporate Service Provider — ACSP).
- Transition period: existing directors must verify within a period to be specified by regulations (expected 2025-2026 phased rollout).
- Failure to verify: Companies House may annotate the register; persistent failure may lead to removal from the register and criminal sanctions.

### Failure to Prevent Fraud (s.199 ECCTA 2023)

- New corporate offence: a large organisation is guilty if an associated person commits a fraud offence intending to benefit the organisation, and the organisation did not have reasonable fraud prevention procedures in place.
- "Large organisation": meets two of three — turnover >£36m, balance sheet >£18m, >250 employees.
- Defence: reasonable fraud prevention procedures or that it was not reasonable to expect such procedures.
- Guidance expected from the Secretary of State (cf. Bribery Act 2010 s.7 model).
- Commencement: 1 September 2025 (SI 2025/115).

### Other Key ECCTA Provisions

- Companies House given new powers to query and reject filings (ss.1-14).
- Registered office must be an "appropriate address" — PO boxes no longer permitted (s.28).
- Lawfulness of purpose: registrar may refuse registration if company appears formed for unlawful purpose (s.3 ECCTA).
- Register of overseas entities strengthened (s.161+).

## 7. Insolvency Procedures

### Administration (Insolvency Act 1986 Schedule B1)

- Purpose hierarchy (para 3):
  1. Rescue the company as a going concern.
  2. Achieve a better result for creditors as a whole than winding up.
  3. Realise property to make a distribution to secured or preferential creditors.
- Appointment routes: court order (para 12), out-of-court by holder of qualifying floating charge (para 14), or by company/directors (para 22).
- Moratorium on legal proceedings (para 43).
- Administrator is an officer of the court (para 5); must be a licensed insolvency practitioner.
- Duration: 12 months, extendable by court or creditor consent.

### Company Voluntary Arrangement (CVA) (IA 1986 Part I ss.1-7B)

- Proposal by directors (or administrator/liquidator) to creditors for a composition or scheme.
- Approved by 75% by value of creditors voting (s.4A).
- Supervised by a nominee (licensed insolvency practitioner).
- Binds all creditors entitled to vote (including dissenting ones), but not secured or preferential creditors without consent (s.4(3)-(4)).

### Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation (CVL) (IA 1986 ss.84-106)

- Directors conclude company cannot continue due to liabilities.
- Special resolution of members (s.84(1)(b)).
- Directors must make a statement of affairs (s.99).
- Liquidator appointed by creditors (s.100).
- Director declaration of solvency: if absent, it is a CVL (not a Members' Voluntary Liquidation).

### Wrongful Trading (s.214 IA 1986)

- Applies where a director knew or ought to have concluded that there was no reasonable prospect of avoiding insolvent liquidation/administration.
- Court may declare the director personally liable to contribute to the company's assets.
- Dual objective/subjective test (similar to s.174 CA 2006 standard).
- Defence: director took every step to minimise potential loss to creditors (s.214(3)).
- COVID-19 suspension (Corporate Insolvency and Governance Act 2020) now expired.

### Fraudulent Trading (s.213 IA 1986)

- Carrying on business with intent to defraud creditors or for any fraudulent purpose.
- Higher threshold than wrongful trading — requires actual dishonesty.
- Both civil (s.213) and criminal (s.993 CA 2006) liability.
- No defence of reasonable behaviour.

### Striking Off vs Dissolution

| Feature | Striking Off (s.1000/s.1003 CA 2006) | Dissolution after Liquidation |
|---------|---------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Route | Company or registrar applies | Automatic after liquidation concludes |
| Cost | £33 (Form DS01) | Included in liquidation process |
| Requirement | Company inactive, no debts, no assets, not threatened with litigation | Formal insolvency process completed |
| Restoration | Administrative (s.1024) within 6 years or court (s.1029) | Court application (s.1029) |
| Risk | Creditors may object; bona vacantia — assets vest in Crown (s.1012) | Assets already distributed |

Voluntary striking off (s.1003): directors apply on Form DS01; company must not have traded or changed name in last 3 months; must notify all members, creditors, employees, directors, and any manager of an employee pension fund.

## 8. Practical Checklists

### New Company Formation Checklist

1. Choose company name (check Companies House name availability; avoid sensitive words — s.55, SI 2014/3140).
2. Determine registered office address (must be in England & Wales, Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland matching registration).
3. Decide on bespoke or Model Articles.
4. Complete Form IN01 with subscriber details, director(s), secretary (optional for private), SIC code, PSC information.
5. Pay registration fee.
6. On incorporation: set up statutory registers (or elect central register at Companies House), issue share certificates within 2 months (s.769), register for Corporation Tax within 3 months.

### Annual Compliance Calendar (Private Ltd)

| Obligation | Deadline | Penalty for Default |
|------------|----------|-------------------|
| Annual accounts filed | 9 months after year-end | £150-£1,500 (doubles for repeat) |
| Confirmation statement | 12 months from last statement/incorporation | Criminal offence; striking off |
| Corporation Tax return | 12 months after accounting period end | Flat penalties + tax-geared penalties |
| Corporation Tax payment | 9 months + 1 day after period end | Interest from due date |
| PAYE/NIC | 22nd of following month (electronic) | Penalties from month 2 |
| VAT return (quarterly) | 1 month + 7 days after quarter end | Default surcharge / points-based regime |
